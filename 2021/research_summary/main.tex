	% The first study analyses the rhetorical strategies of the pipeline industry from a \textit{greenwashing} perspective \citep{Lyon2015}. The pipeline industry has a generous social license that--hundreds of individual companies are allowed operate facilities that carry enormous risks for the local environment. The US experiences over 100 significant\footnote{The Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration describes a pipeline spill as significant if the spill volume exceeds 50 barrels, or one of three other criteria is met--see \url{https://www.phmsa.dot.gov/data-and-statistics/pipeline/pipeline-incident-flagged-files}, accessed 2021-05-12.} pipeline spills every year.\footnote{See \url{https://www.jbarg.net/incident_dashboard.html}, accessed 2021-05-12.} To maintain its social license, and expand its footprint, the industry has to navigate a contradiction. The industry has to present a technology with obvious risks as perfectly controllable and safe. How does the industry, and individual operators navigate this rhetorical challenge?
